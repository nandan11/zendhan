Financial Terms

Understanding the language of finance to make better investment decisions

Common Financial Terms

Understanding these key financial terms will help you navigate the investment landscape with confidence. Below are the most important terms you should know before making investment decisions.

A

Absolute Return: The total return on an investment over a fixed period.

Active Investing: A hands-on approach to investment with frequent buying and selling.

Actuary: A financial expert who assesses risk using mathematics and statistics.

Alpha: A measure of a stock or fundâ€™s excess return over its benchmark index.

Amortization: Gradual reduction of a debt over time through regular payments.

Annual Report: A financial document summarizing a company's performance.

Arbitrage: Profit from price differences of an asset in different markets.

Asset Class: A group of investments with similar characteristics (stocks, bonds, real estate).

Automatic Reinvestment: Automatically reinvesting dividends or interest earned.

Asset Under Managenent (AUM): The total money that people have invested in the fund. Bigger AUM means more trust but doesn't always mean better returns.

B

Balanced Fund: A mutual fund investing in both stocks and bonds.

Bank Reconciliation: Matching personal records with bank statements.

Bankruptcy: Legal process when a person or company cannot repay debts.

Barter System: Exchange of goods/services without using money.

Base Rate: Minimum lending rate set by a central bank.

Bearish Trend: A prolonged decline in stock prices.

Beta: Measures a stock's volatility compared to the overall market.

Bid Price: The highest price a buyer is willing to pay for a stock.

Blockchain: A decentralized digital ledger used in cryptocurrency.

Blue Ocean Strategy: Creating new market demand rather than competing in an existing market.

Bonus Issue: Free additional shares issued by a company to existing shareholders.

Book Building: Process of determining the price of an IPO through investor demand.

Break-Even Point: When total revenues equal total costs.

Brokerage Fee: The commission charged by a broker for executing trades.

Bullish Trend: A sustained increase in stock prices.

Buy and Hold Strategy: Long-term investment strategy ignoring short-term fluctuations.

C

Callable Bond: A bond that can be repaid by the issuer before maturity.

Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR): Measures a bank's capital strength.

Capital Market: A financial market for buying/selling stocks and bonds.

Cash Credit: A short-term loan given to businesses.

Clearing House: An intermediary ensuring smooth financial transactions.

Closing Price: The final price of a stock at the end of a trading day.

Collateralized Debt Obligation (CDO): A pooled investment backed by debt assets.

Commodity Market: A market where raw materials like gold and oil are traded.

Compounding Frequency: The number of times interest is applied to an account per year.

Consumer Credit: Credit given to consumers for buying goods/services.

Convertible Bond: A bond that can be converted into company shares.

Corporate Governance: The system of rules controlling a company.

Coupon Rate: The interest rate paid by a bond annually.

Credit Card APR: Interest rate charged on unpaid credit card balances.

Credit Default Swap (CDS): A financial contract that transfers credit risk.

Credit Utilization Ratio: Percentage of available credit a borrower is using.

Cryptocurrency: A digital currency secured by cryptography (e.g., Bitcoin, Ethereum).

Current Ratio: A financial ratio indicating a company's liquidity.

Custodial Account: An account managed by a guardian for a minor.

D

Debenture: A type of unsecured loan issued by companies.

Debt Consolidation: Combining multiple loans into one for easier repayment.

Default Risk: The risk that a borrower fails to make loan payments.

Deflation: A decrease in the general price levels of goods/services.

Depreciation Rate: The rate at which an asset loses value over time.

Digital Banking: Online banking services without physical branch visits.

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF): A method to estimate the value of an investment.

Dividend Yield: Dividend per share divided by stock price.

Dollar Peg: A currency's fixed exchange rate to the U.S. dollar.

Dow Jones Index: A major stock market index in the U.S.

Dynamic Bond Fund: A bond fund adjusting portfolio based on interest rate changes.

E

Earnings Per Share (EPS): A company's net profit divided by the number of shares.

Economic Moat: A company's competitive advantage over rivals.

E-Gold: A digital version of gold investment.

Emergency Credit Line: A pre-approved loan facility for urgent needs.

Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP): A program where employees get company shares.

Exchange Rate: The value of one currency in terms of another.

F

Financial Derivatives: Contracts deriving value from underlying assets like stocks or bonds.

Financial Inclusion: The availability of financial services to all individuals.

Fintech: Technology-driven financial services.

Fixed-Income Securities: Investments offering fixed periodic interest.

Forex Trading: Trading currencies in the foreign exchange market.

Fractional Investing: Buying a fraction of an expensive stock.

Free Cash Flow: Cash available to a company after operating expenses.

G

GDP Growth Rate: The percentage change in a country's GDP over time.

Green Investing: Investing in companies focused on sustainability.

Gross Margin: Revenue minus the cost of goods sold.

Growth Fund: A mutual fund focusing on stocks with high growth potential.

H

High-Frequency Trading (HFT): Automated trading at high speeds.

HRA (House Rent Allowance): A tax-exempt portion of salary for rented accommodations.

Hybrid Fund: A mix of equity and debt investments in a mutual fund.

I

Index Rebalancing: Adjusting the weight of assets in an index.

Inflation Hedge: An investment that protects against inflation (e.g., gold, real estate).

Insider Trading: Buying/selling stocks using confidential information.

Interest Coverage Ratio: A measure of a company's ability to pay interest on debt.
