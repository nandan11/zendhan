Financial Terms

Understanding the language of finance to make better investment decisions

Common Financial Terms

Understanding these key financial terms will help you navigate the investment landscape with confidence. Below are the most important terms you should know before making investment decisions.

A

401(k) Plan

A retirement savings plan in the U.S.

For example, an employee earning $50,000 annually might contribute 5% ($2,500) to their 401(k), with their employer matching up to 3% ($1,500).

Absolute Return

The total return on an investment over a fixed period.

For example, if you invest $10,000 and after one year it's worth $11,000, your absolute return is $1,000 or 10%.

Active Investing

A hands-on approach to investment with frequent buying and selling.

For example, a day trader might buy stocks in the morning and sell them before the market closes based on price movements.

Actuary

A financial expert who assesses risk using mathematics and statistics.

For example, an actuary helps insurance companies calculate how much to charge for life insurance based on age, health, and lifestyle factors.

Advance Tax

Paying taxes in installments before the financial year ends.

For example, a freelancer earning $80,000 annually might pay $5,000 in quarterly tax installments instead of one large sum at year-end.

Amortization

Gradual reduction of a debt over time through regular payments.

For example, a 30-year mortgage is paid off through monthly payments that include both principal and interest.

Annual Report

A financial document summarizing a company's performance.

For example, Apple's annual report shows its yearly revenue, profit margins, and growth plans for investors to review.

Arbitrage

Profit from price differences of an asset in different markets.

For example, buying gold in one country at $1,800 per ounce and immediately selling it in another country at $1,850 per ounce.

Asset Class

A group of investments with similar characteristics (stocks, bonds, real estate).

For example, an investor might allocate 60% of their portfolio to stocks, 30% to bonds, and 10% to real estate.

Automatic Reinvestment

Automatically reinvesting dividends or interest earned.

For example, instead of receiving a $50 quarterly dividend payment in cash, an investor might choose to automatically buy more shares.

Asset Under Management (AUM)

The total money that people have invested in the fund. Bigger AUM means more trust but doesn't always mean better returns.

For example, Vanguard manages over $7 trillion in assets, which reflects the collective investments of millions of clients.

B

Balanced Fund

A mutual fund investing in both stocks and bonds.

For example, a balanced fund might invest 60% in stocks for growth and 40% in bonds for stability and income.

Bank Reconciliation

Matching personal records with bank statements.

For example, comparing your checkbook balance of $1,250 with your bank statement showing $1,300 might reveal an uncashed check for $50.

Bankruptcy

Legal process when a person or company cannot repay debts.

For example, a business that cannot pay its $500,000 debt might file for Chapter 11 bankruptcy to reorganize and negotiate with creditors.

Barter System

Exchange of goods/services without using money.

For example, a farmer might trade 10 dozen eggs for dental work instead of paying with money.

Base Rate

Minimum lending rate set by a central bank.

For example, when a central bank sets the base rate at 3%, commercial banks typically offer savings accounts at slightly higher rates and loans at even higher rates.

Bearish Trend

A prolonged decline in stock prices.

For example, if the stock market falls consistently over six months, losing 20% of its value, it would be considered a bearish trend.

Beta

Measures a stock's volatility compared to the overall market.

For example, a stock with a beta of 1.5 would typically move 15% when the market moves 10%.

Bid Price

The highest price a buyer is willing to pay for a stock.

For example, if the highest bid for Apple stock is $150, that's the most someone is currently willing to pay per share.

Blockchain

A decentralized digital ledger used in cryptocurrency.

For example, Bitcoin transactions are recorded on a blockchain that shows when each Bitcoin was transferred from one wallet to another.

Blue Ocean Strategy

Creating new market demand rather than competing in an existing market.

For example, Netflix created a blue ocean by offering streaming services when competitors were focused on DVD rentals.

Bonus Issue

Free additional shares issued by a company to existing shareholders.

For example, a company might issue one free share for every five shares already owned, increasing an investor's holdings from 100 to 120 shares without any additional cost.

Book Building

Process of determining the price of an IPO through investor demand.

For example, when Airbnb went public, underwriters gathered investor interest at various price points to determine the final IPO price of $68 per share.

Break-Even Point

When total revenues equal total costs.

For example, if a business spends $100,000 on costs and sells products for $50 each, it needs to sell 2,000 units to break even.

Brokerage Fee

The commission charged by a broker for executing trades.

For example, a broker might charge $6.99 for each stock trade or 0.5% of the transaction value.

Bullish Trend

A sustained increase in stock prices.

For example, if technology stocks rise 25% over several months with steady upward momentum, this represents a bullish trend.

Buy and Hold Strategy

Long-term investment strategy ignoring short-term fluctuations.

For example, an investor might purchase shares of established companies like Coca-Cola and hold them for decades, regardless of short-term market fluctuations.

C

Callable Bond

A bond that can be repaid by the issuer before maturity.

Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR)

Measures a bank's capital strength.

Capital Market

A financial market for buying/selling stocks and bonds.

Cash Credit

A short-term loan given to businesses.

Clearing House

An intermediary ensuring smooth financial transactions.

Closing Price

The final price of a stock at the end of a trading day.

Collateralized Debt Obligation (CDO)

A pooled investment backed by debt assets.

Commodity Market

A market where raw materials like gold and oil are traded.

Examples include markets for gold, silver, crude oil, natural gas, wheat, and coffee.

Compounding Frequency

The number of times interest is applied to an account per year.

Consumer Credit

Credit given to consumers for buying goods/services.

Convertible Bond

A bond that can be converted into company shares.

Corporate Governance

The system of rules controlling a company.

Coupon Rate

The interest rate paid by a bond annually.

Credit Card APR

Interest rate charged on unpaid credit card balances.

Credit Default Swap (CDS)

A financial contract that transfers credit risk.

Credit Utilization Ratio

Percentage of available credit a borrower is using.

For example, if you have a $10,000 credit limit and use $3,000, your credit utilization ratio is 30%.

Cryptocurrency

A digital currency secured by cryptography (e.g., Bitcoin, Ethereum).

Current Ratio

A financial ratio indicating a company's liquidity.

Custodial Account

An account managed by a guardian for a minor.

D

Debenture

A type of unsecured loan issued by companies.

Debt Consolidation

Combining multiple loans into one for easier repayment.

This often helps reduce the overall interest rate and simplifies monthly payments.

Default Risk

The risk that a borrower fails to make loan payments.

Deflation

A decrease in the general price levels of goods/services.

Depreciation Rate

The rate at which an asset loses value over time.

Digital Banking

Online banking services without physical branch visits.

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

A method to estimate the value of an investment.

Dividend Yield

Dividend per share divided by stock price.

Dollar Peg

A currency's fixed exchange rate to the U.S. dollar.

Dow Jones Index

A major stock market index in the U.S.

Dynamic Bond Fund

A bond fund adjusting portfolio based on interest rate changes.

E

Earnings Per Share (EPS)

A company's net profit divided by the number of shares.

EPS is a key indicator used to determine a company's profitability and is often used in price-to-earnings ratios.

Economic Moat

A company's competitive advantage over rivals.

E-Gold

A digital version of gold investment.

Emergency Credit Line

A pre-approved loan facility for urgent needs.

Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP)

A program where employees get company shares.

Exchange Rate

The value of one currency in terms of another.

F

Financial Derivatives

Contracts deriving value from underlying assets like stocks or bonds.

Financial Inclusion

The availability of financial services to all individuals.

Fintech

Technology-driven financial services.

Examples include mobile payment apps, robo-advisors, and blockchain-based financial solutions.

Fixed-Income Securities

Investments offering fixed periodic interest.

Forex Trading

Trading currencies in the foreign exchange market.

Fractional Investing

Buying a fraction of an expensive stock.

Free Cash Flow

Cash available to a company after operating expenses.

G

GDP Growth Rate

The percentage change in a country's GDP over time.

Green Investing

Investing in companies focused on sustainability.

This includes investments in renewable energy, clean technology, and environmentally responsible businesses.

Gross Margin

Revenue minus the cost of goods sold.

Growth Fund

A mutual fund focusing on stocks with high growth potential.

H

High-Frequency Trading (HFT)

Automated trading at high speeds.

HRA (House Rent Allowance)

A tax-exempt portion of salary for rented accommodations.

Hybrid Fund

A mix of equity and debt investments in a mutual fund.

These funds provide both growth potential from stocks and stability from bonds, balancing risk and return.

I

Index Rebalancing

Adjusting the weight of assets in an index.

Inflation Hedge

An investment that protects against inflation (e.g., gold, real estate).

During periods of high inflation, these investments typically maintain or increase their value.

Insider Trading

Buying/selling stocks using confidential information.

Interest Coverage Ratio

A measure of a company's ability to pay interest on debt.
