Common Financial Terms

Understanding these key financial terms will help you navigate the investment landscape in India with confidence. Below are the most important terms you should know before making investment decisions.

A

Absolute Return

The total return on an investment over a fixed period.

For example, if you invest ₹10,000 and after one year it's worth ₹11,000, your absolute return is ₹1,000 or 10%.

Active Investing

A hands-on approach to investment with frequent buying and selling, aiming to outperform a benchmark.

For example, a fund manager actively choosing stocks based on research and market trends.

Actuary

A financial expert who assesses risk using mathematics and statistics, often in insurance.

For example, an actuary helps insurance companies calculate premiums based on life expectancy and risk factors.

Advance Tax

Paying income tax in installments throughout the financial year instead of a lump sum at the end. Applicable in India if your tax liability exceeds a certain limit.

For example, a freelancer earning income liable for tax might pay advance tax quarterly.

AMC (Asset Management Company)

The company that manages the mutual fund and makes investment decisions.

Amortization

Gradual reduction of a debt over time through regular payments, or spreading the cost of an intangible asset over its useful life.

For example, a home loan is paid off through EMIs that include both principal and interest.

Annual Report

A comprehensive financial document published yearly by a company, summarizing its performance, financial health, and future prospects for shareholders and stakeholders.

For example, Reliance Industries' annual report shows its yearly revenue, profit, and strategic initiatives.

Arbitrage

Simultaneously buying and selling an asset in different markets to profit from a price difference. Arbitrage opportunities are usually short-lived.

For example, buying a stock on NSE at ₹100 and selling it on BSE at ₹100.50 almost instantly.

Asset Allocation

The strategy of dividing investments among different asset types (like equity, debt, gold) based on investment goals and risk tolerance.

Asset Class

A group of investments with similar characteristics, risks, and returns (e.g., equities, bonds, real estate, commodities).

For example, an investor might allocate 60% of their portfolio to equities, 30% to bonds (debt), and 10% to gold.

Asset Under Management (AUM)

The total market value of all the financial assets that a mutual fund scheme or financial institution manages on behalf of its investors.

For example, a mutual fund scheme having ₹5,000 crore AUM means it manages investments worth that amount.

Automatic Reinvestment

Automatically using dividends or interest earned from an investment to buy more units or shares of the same investment.

For example, choosing the Growth option in a mutual fund automatically reinvests any profits.

B

Balanced Fund

Also known as Hybrid Funds, these mutual funds invest in a mix of equity (stocks) and debt (bonds) instruments.

For example, a balanced advantage fund dynamically adjusts its allocation between stocks and bonds based on market conditions.

Bank Reconciliation

The process of comparing the cash balance on a company's balance sheet to the corresponding amount on its bank statement to identify differences.

For example, matching transactions recorded in company books with the bank passbook.

Bankruptcy

A legal status of a person or entity that cannot repay debts owed to creditors. In India, this is governed by the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC).

For example, a company undergoing insolvency proceedings under IBC due to inability to pay lenders.

Barter System

A system of exchange where goods or services are directly exchanged for other goods or services without using a medium of exchange, such as money.

For example, historically, a farmer might trade grain for tools.

Base Rate

The minimum interest rate set by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) below which banks were not allowed to lend (superseded by MCLR and External Benchmarks).

For example, older loans might still be linked to a Base Rate.

Bearish Trend / Bear Market

A market condition characterized by falling prices and widespread pessimism. Typically defined as a drop of 20% or more from recent highs.

For example, if the Nifty 50 index falls consistently over months, signaling a bear market.

Benchmark Index

A standard index (like Nifty 50 or Sensex) against which the performance of a mutual fund or investment portfolio is measured.

Beta

A measure of a stock's or mutual fund's volatility (price fluctuation) in relation to the overall market (benchmark index).

A beta greater than 1 suggests higher volatility than the market; less than 1 indicates lower volatility.

Bid Price

The highest price a buyer is willing to pay for a security (like a stock) at a particular time.

For example, if the bid price for Infosys stock is ₹1500, that's the highest current offer to buy.

Blockchain

A decentralized, distributed, and immutable digital ledger used to record transactions across many computers.

For example, cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin use blockchain technology to record transactions securely.

Blue Ocean Strategy

A business strategy focused on creating new market space ("blue oceans") rather than competing in existing, crowded markets ("red oceans").

For example, Cirque du Soleil created a new market combining circus and theater elements.

Blue-Chip Stocks

Stocks of large, well-established, and financially sound companies with a history of reliable performance. Often leaders in their industries.

Bond

A type of loan where an investor lends money to an entity (government or corporation) which borrows the funds for a defined period at a variable or fixed interest rate. Debt mutual funds primarily invest in bonds.

Bonus Issue

An issue of free additional shares given by a company to its existing shareholders, usually in a fixed ratio to their holdings.

For example, a 1:1 bonus issue means an investor gets one additional share for every share they hold.

Book Building

A process used in Initial Public Offerings (IPOs) to determine the offer price based on demand received from institutional investors within a specified price band.

For example, during an IPO, investors bid for shares within a price range (e.g., ₹500-₹520), helping determine the final issue price.

Break-Even Point

The point at which total revenues equal total costs or expenses. There is no net loss or gain.

For example, if a cafe's monthly costs are ₹50,000 and the average sale is ₹100, it needs 500 sales to break even.

Brokerage Fee

The commission charged by a stockbroker for executing buy or sell orders for an investor.

For example, a discount broker in India might charge a flat fee of ₹20 per executed order.

Bullish Trend / Bull Market

A market condition characterized by rising prices and widespread optimism. Investors expect prices to continue increasing.

For example, if the Sensex rises consistently over several months, indicating strong investor confidence.

Buy and Hold Strategy

A long-term passive investment strategy where investors buy securities and hold them for an extended period, regardless of short-term market fluctuations.

For example, buying shares of strong companies and holding them for 10+ years to benefit from long-term growth.

C

CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate)

The average annual growth rate of an investment over a specified period longer than one year, assuming profits are reinvested.

Callable Bond

A type of bond that allows the issuer to repay the bondholder the principal amount before the official maturity date.

Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR)

A measure of a bank's available capital expressed as a percentage of its risk-weighted credit exposures. Used to protect depositors and promote financial stability.

Capital Gains/Losses

The profit or loss made from selling assets like mutual fund units, stocks, or property. In India, these are taxed as Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) or Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) based on the holding period.

Capital Market

Financial markets where long-term debt (over a year) or equity-backed securities are bought and sold. Includes stock markets (like NSE, BSE) and bond markets.

Cash Credit

A short-term source of financing for a company, allowing it to withdraw money from a bank account up to an approved limit.

Clearing House

An intermediary between buyers and sellers of financial instruments (like stocks or derivatives) that guarantees the transaction, reducing counterparty risk.

Closed-End Fund

A mutual fund scheme with a fixed number of units issued during a New Fund Offer (NFO). Units are typically traded on stock exchanges after the NFO, not bought/sold directly with the AMC daily.

Closing Price

The final price at which a security is traded on a given trading day.

Collateralized Debt Obligation (CDO)

A complex structured financial product backed by a pool of loans and other assets.

Commodity Market

A market where raw materials or primary agricultural products like gold, silver, crude oil, wheat, and cotton are traded.

India has exchanges like MCX (Multi Commodity Exchange) for trading commodities.

Compounding Frequency

The number of times per year that accumulated interest is added back to the principal amount, thus earning interest itself.

Consumer Credit

Debt that a person incurs when purchasing goods or services for personal consumption (e.g., credit cards, personal loans).

Convertible Bond

A type of bond that the holder can convert into a specified number of shares of common stock in the issuing company.

Corporate Governance

The system of rules, practices, and processes by which a company is directed and controlled. It balances the interests of stakeholders like shareholders, management, customers, and the community.

Coupon Rate

The annual interest rate paid by the issuer of a bond relative to the bond's face value (par value).

Credit Card APR

The Annual Percentage Rate charged on your credit card balance if you don't pay it off in full each month.

Credit Default Swap (CDS)

A financial derivative that allows an investor to "swap" or offset their credit risk with that of another investor.

Credit Utilization Ratio

The percentage of your total available credit limit that you are currently using. A lower ratio is generally better for your credit score.

For example, if you have a total credit limit of ₹1,00,000 across all cards and use ₹30,000, your utilization ratio is 30%.

Cryptocurrency

A digital or virtual currency secured by cryptography, making it nearly impossible to counterfeit. Examples include Bitcoin and Ethereum.

Current Ratio

A liquidity ratio that measures a company's ability to pay short-term obligations (due within one year). Calculated as Current Assets / Current Liabilities.

Custodial Account

A financial account (like a bank or investment account) set up and managed by an adult (custodian) for a minor (beneficiary).

D

Debenture

A type of long-term debt instrument issued by companies and governments that is not secured by physical assets or collateral.

Debt Consolidation

Combining multiple debts (like credit card balances or personal loans) into a single, larger debt, often with a lower interest rate or monthly payment.

Taking a single personal loan to pay off several credit card dues.

Debt Funds

Mutual funds that primarily invest in fixed-income securities like government bonds, corporate bonds, and money market instruments. Generally considered lower risk than equity funds.

Default Risk

The risk that a borrower will be unable to make required payments on their debt obligation (interest or principal).

Deflation

A decrease in the general price level of goods and services, opposite of inflation. Money becomes worth more over time.

Demat Account

An account to hold financial securities (like stocks, bonds, ETFs, mutual fund units) in electronic form. Necessary for trading on Indian stock exchanges.

Depreciation Rate

The rate at which an asset (like machinery or a vehicle) loses its value over time due to wear and tear or obsolescence.

Digital Banking

Providing banking services through online platforms (websites, mobile apps) rather than physical branches.

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

A valuation method used to estimate the value of an investment based on its expected future cash flows, discounted back to their present value.

Diversification

The strategy of spreading investments across various asset classes, industries, or geographical regions to reduce overall risk. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket." Mutual funds inherently offer diversification.

Dividend/IDCW (Income Distribution cum Capital Withdrawal)

Profits distributed by a company (as stock dividends) or a mutual fund scheme to its unitholders. Mutual fund investors can choose a Growth option (reinvest profits) or IDCW option (receive payouts).

Dividend Yield

A financial ratio showing how much a company pays out in dividends each year relative to its stock price. Calculated as Annual Dividend Per Share / Stock Price Per Share.

Dollar Peg

Fixing a country's currency exchange rate to the U.S. dollar.

Dow Jones Index (DJIA)

The Dow Jones Industrial Average, a major stock market index representing 30 large, publicly-owned companies based in the United States.

Dynamic Bond Fund

A type of debt mutual fund where the fund manager actively adjusts the portfolio's average maturity based on interest rate forecasts.

E

Earnings Per Share (EPS)

A company's net profit divided by the number of its outstanding common shares. Indicates profitability per share.

A higher EPS generally indicates better profitability and value for shareholders.

Economic Moat

A company's distinct competitive advantage that allows it to protect its market share and profitability from competitors (term popularized by Warren Buffett).

E-Gold

Investing in gold in electronic form rather than physical gold. Can be done through Gold ETFs or Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) in India.

ELSS (Equity Linked Savings Scheme)

A type of equity mutual fund that offers tax benefits under Section 80C of the Income Tax Act in India, with a mandatory lock-in period of 3 years.

Emergency Credit Line

A pre-approved loan facility provided by banks or financial institutions that can be accessed quickly during financial emergencies.

Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP)

A benefit plan that gives employees an ownership interest in the company, usually in the form of stock options.

Equity / Equity Funds

Equity represents ownership in a company (stocks or shares). Equity mutual funds primarily invest in stocks, offering potential for higher returns but also carrying higher risk.

ETF (Exchange Traded Fund)

A type of investment fund, often tracking an index (like Nifty 50), that is traded on stock exchanges like regular stocks. Requires a Demat account.

Exchange Rate

The value of one country's currency expressed in terms of another country's currency (e.g., USD/INR rate).

Exit Load

A fee charged by some mutual fund schemes if investors redeem (sell) their units before a specified holding period has passed.

Expense Ratio

An annual fee charged by the AMC to cover the fund's operating and management expenses, expressed as a percentage of the fund's average AUM. It directly impacts investor returns.

F

Financial Derivatives

Financial contracts whose value is derived from an underlying asset, group of assets, or benchmark (e.g., futures, options).

Financial Inclusion

Efforts to make formal financial services (banking, credit, insurance) accessible and affordable to all segments of society.

Fintech

Financial Technology: The use of technology to improve and automate the delivery and use of financial services.

Examples include UPI payment apps (Paytm, Google Pay), online investment platforms (Zerodha, Groww), robo-advisors.

Fixed-Income Securities

Investments that provide returns in the form of fixed periodic interest payments and the eventual return of principal at maturity (e.g., bonds, fixed deposits).

FMP (Fixed Maturity Plan)

Generally closed-end debt mutual funds where the investment tenure is fixed, and the portfolio consists of bonds maturing around the same time as the scheme's tenure.

Forex Trading

Foreign Exchange Trading: The buying and selling of currencies in the global foreign exchange market.

Fractional Investing

Buying a portion or fraction of a single share of an expensive stock, making high-priced stocks more accessible.

Free Cash Flow

The cash a company generates after accounting for cash outflows to support operations and maintain its capital assets. Indicates financial flexibility.

Fund Manager

The professional responsible for implementing a mutual fund's investment strategy and managing its portfolio holdings.

G

GDP Growth Rate

Gross Domestic Product Growth Rate: The percentage increase in the total market value of all final goods and services produced in a country over a specific period.

Green Investing

Investing in companies or projects that are committed to environmental sustainability and conservation.

Includes investments in renewable energy, waste management, and sustainable agriculture businesses.

Gross Margin

A company's total revenue minus its cost of goods sold (COGS), expressed as a percentage. Indicates efficiency in production.

Growth Fund

A type of mutual fund (usually equity) that focuses on investing in companies expected to grow at an above-average rate compared to others.

Growth Option

An option in mutual fund schemes where profits (dividends or capital gains) earned by the fund are automatically reinvested back into the scheme, leading to appreciation in the NAV over time. No payouts are made to the investor.

H

High-Frequency Trading (HFT)

An automated trading platform used by large investment banks, hedge funds, and institutional investors that utilizes powerful computers to transact a large number of orders at extremely high speeds.

HRA (House Rent Allowance)

An allowance provided by employers to employees as part of their salary to cover accommodation expenses. Salaried individuals in India can claim tax exemption on HRA under certain conditions.

Hybrid Fund

A category of mutual funds that invests in a mix of asset classes, typically equity and debt instruments, aiming to provide a balance between growth and stability. Also known as Balanced Funds.

Examples include Aggressive Hybrid Funds (higher equity allocation) and Conservative Hybrid Funds (higher debt allocation).

I

Index Funds

Mutual funds or ETFs that aim to replicate the performance of a specific market index (like Nifty 50 or Sensex) by holding securities in the same proportion as the index. They are passively managed and usually have lower expense ratios.

Index Rebalancing

The process of periodically adjusting the constituents and their weights within a market index to ensure it accurately reflects the market segment it represents.

Inflation Hedge

An investment considered likely to maintain or increase its value over time, thereby protecting against the decrease in purchasing power caused by inflation.

Assets like gold, real estate, and sometimes equities are considered potential inflation hedges.

Insider Trading

The illegal practice of trading on the stock exchange to one's own advantage through having access to confidential, non-public information.

Interest Coverage Ratio

A debt and profitability ratio used to determine how easily a company can pay interest on its outstanding debt. Calculated as Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT) / Interest Expense.

Investment Objective

The primary goal of a mutual fund scheme (e.g., long-term capital appreciation, regular income, capital preservation), which guides its investment strategy and portfolio construction.

J

No terms starting with J currently listed.

K

KYC (Know Your Customer)

A mandatory process used by financial institutions in India to verify the identity and address of their clients before or while conducting financial transactions. Required for investing in mutual funds, opening bank accounts, etc.

L

Liquidity

The ease with which an asset or security can be quickly bought or sold in the market without significantly affecting its price. Open-ended mutual funds generally offer high liquidity.

Lock-in Period

A specified duration during which investors cannot redeem or sell their investments from a particular scheme. Common in tax-saving instruments.

For example, ELSS mutual funds have a mandatory lock-in period of 3 years in India.

Lump Sum Investment

Investing a relatively large amount of money in a mutual fund scheme or other investment at one single point in time, as opposed to investing smaller amounts periodically via SIP.

M

Market Capitalization (Large-Cap, Mid-Cap, Small-Cap)

The total market value of a company's outstanding shares (Current Share Price x Total Number of Outstanding Shares). Companies are categorized based on market cap (e.g., Large-Cap > ₹20,000 Cr, Mid-Cap ₹5,000 Cr - ₹20,000 Cr, Small-Cap < ₹5,000 Cr as per SEBI norms in India). Mutual funds are often classified accordingly.

Mutual Fund

An investment vehicle professionally managed by an Asset Management Company (AMC) that pools money from many investors to purchase a diversified portfolio of securities like stocks, bonds, or other assets.

N

NAV (Net Asset Value)

The per-unit market value of a mutual fund scheme, calculated daily by the AMC. It's derived by dividing the total net assets (total assets minus total liabilities) of the scheme by the total number of units outstanding. Investors buy and sell units at the applicable NAV.

NFO (New Fund Offer)

The first-time subscription offer for units of a new mutual fund scheme being launched by an AMC. During the NFO period, units are typically offered at a face value, often ₹10 per unit.

O

Open-Ended Fund/Scheme

The most common type of mutual fund scheme that is available for subscription and redemption on an ongoing basis. Investors can buy and sell units directly from the AMC on any business day at the prevailing NAV.

P

Portfolio

A collection or group of all financial assets and investments held by an individual investor or a mutual fund scheme. It can include stocks, bonds, mutual funds, real estate, etc.

Q

No terms starting with Q currently listed.

R

Risk / Risk Tolerance

Risk is the possibility that an investment's actual return will differ from its expected return, including the potential loss of some or all of the original investment. Risk tolerance is an investor's ability and willingness to withstand potential losses in their investments.

Rupee Cost Averaging

An investment strategy achieved by investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals (like via SIP). This results in buying more units when prices are low and fewer units when prices are high, potentially lowering the average cost per unit over time.

S

SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India)

The regulatory body for the securities and commodity market in India, established to protect the interests of investors and regulate the securities market, including mutual funds.

SIP (Systematic Investment Plan)

A method offered by mutual funds allowing investors to invest a fixed amount of money automatically at regular intervals (e.g., weekly, monthly, quarterly) in a chosen scheme.

Stock

A type of security representing ownership (equity) in a corporation. Owning stock means having a claim on part of the company's assets and earnings. Equity mutual funds primarily invest in stocks.

STP (Systematic Transfer Plan)

A plan that allows investors to give consent to a mutual fund to periodically transfer (switch) a fixed amount or units from one scheme to another scheme within the same fund house.

SWP (Systematic Withdrawal Plan)

A facility that allows mutual fund investors to withdraw a fixed amount of money from their scheme at regular intervals (e.g., monthly, quarterly) to meet regular income needs.

V

Volatility / Beta

Volatility refers to the degree of variation or fluctuation in the price or NAV of a security or mutual fund over time. Higher volatility generally implies higher risk. Beta is a statistical measure of a fund's volatility relative to its benchmark index.
